1.1 The purpose of IT in contemporary business

IT is the very essence of the modern world, from home-based micro businesses on Etsy or eBay up to corporations on the international stage. Technology is the synapses of economy. Every company no matter how big or small has a website, half a dozen social media accounts, and probably it’s own email server. If the company is of a decent size it could have dozens of internally used software programs to do everything from payroll and invoices, to stock management, human resources, department communications, security protocols, group organisation and calendars.

Multinationals could not function without the ability to correspond instantly between employees all over the planet, advertising firms are heavily invested in social media and digital formats to appeal to a contemporary audience and even the freelance craftsman relies on digital platforms to secure customers and get their name out into the public eye. We are long past the point whether it is debatable, IT is here to stay and the economic digital arms race is shaping up to be never ending.

1.3 Planning and carrying out tasks using IT

I found Trello invaluable when working on the team project, but when working solo I found it unnecessarily slow compared to other methods of organisation. For this project I used it to draft out my plans and tried the ‘Kanban’ method for this project. But as someone who has spent over a decade freelancing I have some very set methods of working when working solo that flows well with my thought processes. I think trello slowed me down, it was annoying to load on the low powered wifi in the college, the app uses quite a bit of memory on my phone and the login process is annoying when moving onto a new device. I then had to shuffle around and edit every single card after I’d done a few hours work, this took a lot more time than usual. I’d definitely use it again for any group project, but for solo work I have better refined processes. The ‘Kanban’ method however was quite useful and I will definitely use that again.

Normally I would have set up a google docs that is automatically linked to my various google accounts and all my devices. I can invite other people to edit it if necessary. Then use a colour coding system to organise what needs doing and when. I type everything that needs doing in black, if something gets done it get changed to red, if something becomes urgent it becomes yellow, if something is waiting for supplies or other prerequisites to be delivered before it can be actioned it becomes blue, then when the prerequisite item comes it gets switched to black or yellow depending if it’s urgent or not. If you do a long day of work all you need to do is highlight the bullet points you have completed and turn them all to red with one click, which is a lot faster than trello was.

I understand you can do similar stuff with the colour tags in trello, but it just seemed a lot more clunky to manipulate especially on mobile. However I would definitely be willing to use trello for group porjects in future. Click here for Trello


1.4 Risk management in digital projects and why so many businesses fail at IT

I grew up listening to a lot of punk music, I’ve still got a bit of an anti authoritarian streak and like to find my own ways to do things more often than not, it’s what makes me a good creative mind and allows me to do what I do.

However I know nobody is bigger than their industry. Unless you become the next Google, Apple or Coca-Cola and have the power to define your industry single handily you will need to bow to the programs and best practices dictated by the millions of people who also work in your field.

I worked as a graphic designer for many years, and every single job posting I’ve ever seen, without exception mentions ability in at least one of the Adobe creative suit programs is a requirement. Mostly Photoshop, illustrator and InDesign. If you want to work in the industry these are the basic tools of your trade. An interior decorator can’t decide he’s suddenly never going to use paint again and only work in wallpaper; he won’t get enough business to survive because wallpaper is nowhere near as popular. You have to bow to the industry standards, whatever they may be in your case. I really like several other image manipulation programs out there more than Photoshop, but at work I use the hell out of Photoshop with style and fluency.

Killing your ego, abandoning personal preferences and using the software or coding language expected by your industry is not always pleasant but it is necessary. I see it as the evolved version of the old saying “There’s a tool for every job and a hammer is often several of them.” When you scale up a working process to the scale required by the digital world, with possibly thousands of employees working on a final product, often across several continents, standardisation is the lubrication that makes the bearings run smoothly.

1.7 Legality and constraints in digital landscapes

The law surrounding digital practices is incredibly intricate and complex with each country or major region having its own idea of what is and isn’t allowed in their territory. Europe and the UK have very stringent laws about data protection, data collection and freedom of information. Not to mention the minefields that are the out-dated and inefficient copyright laws in a world of unlimited hosting, countless platforms, instant sharing and lightning speed, fluid social media.

On the international stage, businesses dedicate entire departments of lawyers and digital experts to navigating the various laws and processes of performing in a digital space in the bounds of a specific territory. China is the most notable example of this, with Hollywood films and multinational games developers often censoring their own products to get past Chinese sensors. The buying power of the country is too big to discount off hand and companies may go to extreme lengths to appease the stringent and aggressive demands of the Chinese government, often putting themselves in no win situations.

The best example of this is the Blizzard fiasco of October 2019. In the third month of Honk Kong freedom protests, the Hearthstone (a spotlight blizzard game) Grandmasters (their flagship tournament) winner Blitzchung used his acceptance speech to draw attention to the plight of the protesters in Hong Kong. Blizzard cut the stream, banned him from competing, rescinded his prize money and even went as far as firing the two shoutcasters (eSports commentators) who were interviewing him. The backlash was immediate, at time of writing thousands of people have deleted their blizzard accounts and there is a boycott underway, they have had large staff walkouts and are stuck between their western audience who value free speech and the Chinese sensors. Whatever they do they risk loosing a financially significant amount of their player base.

Interestingly the disenchanted player base has started to try and get Blizzard banned in china anyway by taking one of the main characters from Overwatch (their current flagship eSport) who is Chinese and depicting her in Hong Kong flags wearing the masks and iconography that is common to the anti-Chinese protesters. If this attempt to turn her into an icon of anti-Chinese rebellion is successful and China adds the character Mei to their list of banned symbols (along with Winnie the Pooh and other fictional characters), Blizzard could face their biggest game being banned in China anyway or have to remove one of the most loved and profitable characters from their biggest game. Either way they will still be loosing massive credibility with their western audience. They’ve really stepped on a digital landmine with this one and as someone who has previously been heavily invested in some of their properties it will be fascinating to watch it unfold.

So far Blizzards only response was a tweet apologising for “damaging the dignity of the Chinese people.” And they have disabled the ability to delete your account, now while this does mean they won’t take a huge hit to subscriber amounts when they have to report to their shareholders, many people are pointing out it could very well violate the GDPR in European territories because of laws about data protection and deletion as well as violating some of their own terms and conditions that are signed when a client downloads, purchases or installs one of their products. This could leave them open to class action law suits in Europe and possibly elsewhere. Some people have been getting around this inability to delete their accounts in protest by forcing Blizzard to ban their account by creating aggressively pro Hong Kong and free speech and anti Blizzard and China posts on the official Blizzard message boards and social media.

Other companies have been quick to jump on the bandwagon however, with Redbull releasing a pro Hong Kong advert less than 24 hours after this hit news headlines and several rival gaming companies tweeting support of free speech and one smaller company who make a rival card game to Hearthstone called “Gods Unchained” have been successful at stealing some spotlight by offering to cover the prize money of the Hearthstone player that started it all and job offers for the two newly fired shoutcasters.

As of time of writing this whole thing happened three days ago and the situation has become so well reported that American senators from both the Republicans and the Democrats have publicly denounced Blizzards kowtowing to Chinese censorship. Blizzards stock has dropped 4 points this week.

This is obviously an extreme example, but within the current digital landscape even the smallest action can snowball into the hugest political or legal nightmare for a business.

Update: It's been three days which is an eternity in the world of the internet, Blizzard caved and gave Blitzchung his prize money but kept him banned for six months down from twelve. The shoutcasters are still fired and Blizzards number one caster has resigned in protest. They looked like they were on full damage control, but then released a statement saying anyone found at Blizzcon with any winnie the pooh merchandise at the end of the month, will be escorted off the premesis, Winnie the Pooh being a banned character in China due to the memes making fun of Xi Jinping for looking like said bear. One meme had been trying to get people to turn up in Winnie the Pooh cosplay as a protest, this crackdown has just reignited the fire bigger than ever. One step forwards, two backwards seems to be Blizzards plan for this issue.

In related news the NBA lost millions in sponsorship this morning from Chinese companies pulling out of deals because of a tweet from one coach of one team. You have to wonder if making billion dollar business deals with China is worth it for companies if they can be completely torn down with a single tweet from one minor person in your business. Will markets have to choose between the east or west in future? The western audience seems to be rightiously against China's totalitarian government and mass human rights violations. Can a business bridge the gap and win both sides or are all these attempts to break into the Chinese market a lose/lose eventuality? Time will tell.

1.2.5 Copyright and other constraints effecting websites

In the UK: You get the copyright of any work of creation from moment of creation, this includes everything that could be considered a work of creative practice and has legal precedence to include website layouts and design. You don’t need to add any copyright notice to have your work protected but it is common practice to the copyright symbol (©), your name and the year of creation. This also means you cannot copy others works without permission, however there are exceptions for reasons like non-commercial research, parody, criticism, reviews, reporting current events and more.

In the US: Works are copyrighted from the moment of creation despite the format of publication or mediums used as long as it is fixed in a tangible form – published some how. You don’t need to claim a copyright for it to be in effect (the law eliminated public notice in 1989). They also use the (©) symbol.

1.2.7 Appropraite filetypes for websites

There are literally dozens of appropriate file types depending on what you need to do. For images .jpg is the standard, but .png is great if you want a transparent background, .gif allows you to make moving images that act like pseudo videos. For music and video .mp3 and .mp4 are great respectively. For coding .html and .css are the bulk of front end internet pages. You want .exe for downloading and transferring runnable programs on windows devices. .pdf is standard for documents and very easy to access. .rar and .zip are for compressing large files into more manageable file sizes. Picking the right file type is vital, but there are dozens and dozens of common file types and they all have different applications.

1.2.8 Storing and retrieving files effectively

Firstly you should always zip your large files. What counts as a large file is pretty subjective but if it takes more than about a minute to upload/download then I'd definitely compress it with a .zip or .rar depending on where I plan on sending it. The method of file storage really depends on the sensitivity of the documents involved. Cloud storage is great for most day to day options, and most devices have automatic compression features that just work in the background. These can then be shared, accessed and viewed by you or anyone with an internet connection you share the link and password to. However the cloud is not a safe method of storage, you never know for sure by who or where your data is being hosted. There are entire un-indexed websites (darkweb) that are dedicated in trading files found from compromised cloud accounts. Wikileaks is by far the biggest repository of leaked documents from many different sources, quite a number are dredged from compromised cloud accounts. In 2014 dozens of high profile and A-list Hollywood actresses had their nudes leaked, it made international news and was traced back to compromised iCloud accounts. So the cloud is great, but don't put anything up you wouldn't post on your facebook. I am personally partial to just emailing stuff to my own email, it means I can log on anywhere and just search my inbox for the relevant email and I can download those files to any device. If you just want free storage of small images and files then this is an alright way of doing it. For more secure options there are paid for encrypted storage sites that function similar to the cloud but are more secure. However anything on the internet could in theory be accessed by someone determined enough. But for really confidential files I'd password encrypt it if it had to be sent via the internet, or possibly just keep it on a external hard drive or thumb drive, keep it physically with you or somewhere locked safe.
